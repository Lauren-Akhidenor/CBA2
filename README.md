# Cost–Benefit Analysis of Organic vs Integrated Farming: Amaranth & Corchorus Production in Smallholder Systems  

---

## Project Overview

This project evaluates the **economic performance of Organic vs Integrated farming systems** for two African leafy vegetables:

- Amaranth  
- Corchorus  

The aim is to determine which system delivers better:
- Profitability  
- Cost efficiency  
- Smallholder sustainability  

> 📌 Key Insight: Higher input use does not always lead to higher profit.

---

## Research Objective

- Compare cost structures of both farming systems  
- Evaluate yield and revenue differences  
- Identify the most economically viable system  
- Support smallholder agricultural decision-making  

---

## Analytical Approach

This study applies **Cost–Benefit Analysis (CBA)**:

- Net Benefit = Total Revenue − Total Cost  
- ROI (%) = (Net Benefit ÷ Total Cost) × 100  
- Benefit–Cost Ratio (BCR) = Revenue ÷ Cost  

> ⚠️ Note: NPV and IRR were excluded due to single-season data limitations.

---

## Key Assumptions

- Crop price fixed at ₦200/kg  
- One production cycle (single season)  
- Average weather and pest conditions  
- Labour costs are constant across systems  
- No post-harvest losses included

# ⚠️ Why Integrated Farming Performed Worse

- Higher input costs (fertiliser + pesticide)  
- No proportional yield improvement  
- Possible inefficient input application  
- Diminishing returns to chemical intensification  

---

# Why Organic Farming Performed Better

- Lower dependency on purchased inputs  
- More stable yield outcomes  
- Higher cost efficiency  
- Positive ROI despite lower capital intensity  

---

# Sensitivity Analysis

## Scenario 1: Price drops by 20%
- Organic remains profitable  
- Integrated farming deepens losses  

## Scenario 2: Manure cost increases by 30%
- Organic ROI declines but stays positive  
- Integrated system becomes more negative  

---

# Policy Implications

- Integrated systems require optimisation, not just adoption  
- Organic farming is viable for low-capital farmers  
- Manure cost (~60% of total cost) is a major constraint  
- Extension services should focus on efficiency improvements
